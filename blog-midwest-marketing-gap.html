Most Midwest Contractors Aren't Marketing. That's Your Opening.

By Urban Niche Co. April 22, 2026 7 min read

Industry estimates suggest that somewhere between 30% and 50% of home service contractors across the Midwest don't have a functioning website. Of the ones that do, most look like they were built in 2014 and never touched again.

Now add this: less than half of small contractors have claimed their Google Business Profile. Most haven't responded to a single review in over six months. A huge percentage don't post on social media with any regularity — or at all.

Go look up the top ten roofers in your zip code. Check the fifth, sixth, and seventh result. If you're in most parts of Iowa, Wisconsin, Illinois, Missouri, Minnesota, or Kansas, at least half of them will have:

  • A broken contact form or a form that goes to an inbox nobody checks
  • Reviews that stop in 2022
  • A Facebook page that hasn't been updated in 14 months
  • No blog, no video, no content strategy of any kind
  • A phone number that goes to a voicemail that's been full since winter

This isn't a small gap. This is a wide-open market.

Why The Gap Exists

Contractors aren't lazy — they're the opposite. They're among the hardest-working people in the country. Trade professionals get up at 5am, work in the rain, deal with angry customers, manage payroll, fix their trucks, and still make dinner with their families most nights.

Marketing is just not where their bandwidth goes.

Here's what most Midwest contractors are actually dealing with on a Monday morning:

  • Two call-offs from the crew
  • A rain-delayed project from Friday that needs to finish today
  • A material order that never shipped
  • A supplier that wants payment before they'll release the next order
  • A past customer calling with a warranty question

Writing Instagram captions is not in that list. Updating a website is not in that list. Responding to a two-star review from someone who was mad about pricing three months ago — also not in that list.

So the work piles up. And every week that the phone keeps ringing from word-of-mouth referrals, another week goes by where "I should really work on our marketing" gets pushed to next week.

That's how ten years pass. That's how a business with a 25-year reputation and a beautiful truck fleet ends up invisible online while the guy who started two years ago and takes a Saturday afternoon to post a job site photo each week is outranking them for "[your trade] near me."

What The 5–10% Who Are Serious Are Doing

In every Midwest market, there's a small group — maybe five or ten percent of the contractors — who treat marketing like any other job on the calendar. Not optional, not "when we have time," but scheduled, systematic, and boring.

Here's what they're actually doing:

They post job site photos consistently. Not professional shoots. Just before/afters, from their phones, with a sentence of context. One a week. Sometimes one a day.

They respond to every review — good and bad — within 48 hours. A two-sentence thank you on the five-stars. A calm, factual response to the one-stars. No drama.

Their Google Business Profile is filled out completely. Services listed. Hours right. Categories picked properly. Photos added monthly. Updates posted. Every feature Google gives them, they're using.

Their website loads in under two seconds and tells you in the first screen what they do, where, and how to reach them. That's it. Not flashy. Not artsy. Just clear.

They ask every happy customer for a review. Every single one. Some of them automate it. Some of them send a text at the end of the day. Most of them just ask.

They run a small amount of paid advertising. Not huge budgets. Just enough to show up at the top of Google when someone searches for their service in their zip code.

They have an email list of past customers — maintenance reminders, seasonal reminders, a random referral offer — going out at least once a quarter.

That is the entire playbook. Almost nothing on that list costs significant money. Most of it costs an hour or two a week.

And yet 90–95% of contractors don't do it.

The Math Nobody Talks About

Let's walk through a hypothetical. Pick a Midwest market with 50,000 people. Let's say there are 80 roofers in that market. Of those 80:

  • Maybe 40 have some kind of website.
  • Of those 40, maybe 20 have a site that loads fast, looks modern, and has a working form.
  • Of those 20, maybe 10 consistently appear on page one of Google for their primary service keywords.
  • Of those 10, maybe 5 are also active on social media, responding to reviews, and running a small ad program.

So out of 80 roofers, there are maybe 5 who are actually competing for online leads in a serious way.

Now here's the part that matters: online lead generation is not optional anymore. It's where homeowners under 50 find everyone. It's where homeowners over 50 are heading. Even referral business — the lifeblood of most traditional contracting companies — now flows through Google searches. The homeowner hears about you from their neighbor, and then they Google you. What they see in that first three-second glance determines whether they call.

If your online presence is thin and one of those 5 serious competitors is doing it right, the referred customer now has a reason to call them instead.

That math is going to get worse over the next five years. Not better.

What "Getting Serious" Actually Looks Like

If you're reading this and thinking "yeah, we should probably do more" — the honest truth is that most contractors who say that will not actually do more. Twelve months from now, they'll say the same sentence.

The ones who break out aren't smarter. They're not better at marketing. They're not richer.

They just decided at some point that marketing was going to be a real, recurring part of the business. Like ordering materials. Like processing payroll. Like training a new apprentice.

Here's what "getting serious" tends to look like in practice:

  1. Somebody owns it. Either you, your spouse, a family member who's good at this stuff, a part-time virtual assistant, or an outside firm. But one human, one calendar.
  2. There's a weekly minimum. Five job site photos posted. Two reviews responded to. One Google Business update. One email sent. Hit the minimum even in the busy season. Especially in the busy season.
  3. Results get measured. Not obsessively. Just a monthly number: how many leads came in, where did they come from, how many turned into booked jobs. Over six months, the pattern gets clear.
  4. The stuff that doesn't work gets dropped. Don't write a blog post every week if nobody reads blogs. Don't boost Facebook posts if they don't convert. Double down on what's actually bringing calls.
  5. The boring stuff gets automated. Review requests, email follow-ups, maintenance reminders — these are tasks that get set up once and run for years.

That's the list. There's no trick. The contractors pulling ahead in Midwest markets aren't doing anything special. They're just doing something. Consistently.

The Window Is Real, And It's Closing Slowly

Every month, another contractor in every Midwest market wakes up and decides this is the year they get serious about their online presence. Some of them hire help. Some of them do it themselves. Either way, they start showing up.

The gap is wide right now. It will not be wide forever.

In five years, the contractors dominating their markets will be the ones who started getting systematic in 2025, 2026, 2027. Not because they did anything heroic — just because they started.

The ones who wait until 2029 because "this year is too busy" will be playing catch-up for the rest of the decade. Some of them won't catch up.

The Question Isn't Whether Marketing Works

Marketing works for contractors. This isn't a debate anymore. The question is never "does it work" — every market has proof that it works. The question is always: will you be the one who does it, or will the roofer down the road who started last spring beat you to it?

That's a decision, not a strategy.

And like most important decisions, it's boring. You commit, you schedule it, you stick to it when you don't feel like it, and eighteen months later you wake up with a pipeline that didn't exist before.

The Midwest is wide open. Most of your competition isn't trying. The ones who are trying aren't doing anything special — they're just consistent.

If you've read this far, you know what the work is. The only question left is whether this is the year you actually start.
